We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled **"The KOTHA Framework: a counterfactual simulation and Bayesian integration approach to diagnosing structural information loss in randomized controlled trial meta-analyses"** for consideration for publication in *Research Synthesis Methods*.

**Summary.** We present the KOTHA (Knowledge-driven Observational-Trial Harmonization Approach) Framework, a three-module methodological system that addresses a systematic but underrecognized source of error in evidence synthesis: structural information loss caused by enrollment-driven event dilution in RCTs. The framework comprises (1) Module K, which uses counterfactual power simulation to diagnose and quantify event dilution; (2) Module T, which integrates discordant RCT and observational evidence through hierarchical Bayesian meta-analysis with power-prior discounting; and (3) Module H, which translates quantitative outputs into GRADE-compatible assessments with standardized recommendation language distinguishing "evidence of no effect" from "no evidence of effect."

**Relevance to Research Synthesis Methods.** We believe this manuscript is well suited to the scope of *Research Synthesis Methods* for the following reasons:

1. **Novel synthesis methodology.** The KOTHA Framework introduces a new integrated approach to evidence synthesis that combines counterfactual simulation, Bayesian meta-analysis, and structured guideline interpretation --- three methodological components that have previously been applied in isolation. This directly addresses the journal's focus on advancing methods for research synthesis.

2. **Bayesian evidence integration with transparent sensitivity analysis.** Module T implements power-prior discounting across a dual-parameter transparency grid (discounting parameter $\alpha$ and bias adjustment $\delta$), providing a principled and reproducible approach to combining RCT and observational evidence. This contributes to the growing literature on Bayesian synthesis methods that *Research Synthesis Methods* has been instrumental in advancing.

3. **Empirical validation with real published data.** Unlike purely theoretical proposals, we validate the framework using study-level data from two canonical cases of observational-RCT divergence: intravenous magnesium in acute myocardial infarction (12 trials, 1984--1995) and statins in heart failure (5 observational studies, 2 RCTs). This empirical grounding demonstrates practical applicability.

4. **GRADE framework integration.** Module H maps directly onto established GRADE domains (imprecision, indirectness), providing quantitative inputs through optimal information size (OIS) and trial sequential analysis (TSA). This bridging of statistical methodology and evidence assessment practice is central to the journal's mission of connecting methods development with practical application.

5. **Reproducibility and reporting standards.** The simulation component follows the ADEMP reporting framework (Morris et al., 2019), and all validation code (Python) is provided as supplementary material. The transparency grid approach ensures that readers can evaluate how conclusions depend on specific assumptions.

**Significance.** The framework addresses a critical gap in current evidence synthesis practice: the systematic misinterpretation of underpowered RCT meta-analyses as providing "evidence of no effect." Our empirical validation demonstrates that standard GRADE assessment and KOTHA-enhanced assessment can reach substantively different conclusions for the same evidence base, with direct implications for clinical guideline recommendations. Given the increasing interest in combining randomized and non-randomized evidence --- a topic of active methodological development in the synthesis community --- we believe this work will be of substantial interest to the readership of *Research Synthesis Methods*.

The manuscript has not been published previously and is not under consideration elsewhere. All authors have approved the manuscript and agree with its submission to *Research Synthesis Methods*.
